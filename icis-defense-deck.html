How Does Generative AI Impact Software Developer Performance?

Panel evidence on routinization, faithful appropriation, and task–technology fit across 500 repositories and 1.3 million developer-months

Doctoral Defense Abdou Aziz Camara Université Paris-Dauphine – PSL · EDBA May 2026

The anomaly

The same technology, measured rigorously, produces opposite results.

0%
Faster task completion with AI assistance (Peng et al. 2023, RCT)
−0%
Slower for experienced developers on familiar code (Becker et al. 2025)
0%
Of organizations report no measurable value from AI investment

The 2024 DORA Report documents a historical first: throughput and instability rising together. These contradictions are not noise — they are a signal that our theories lack the vocabulary for a generative, continuously evolving technology.

Why existing IS theory falls short

A conceptual gap, and a managerial vacuum

  • Dominant frameworks assume linear technology–performance relationships and static capabilities — GenAI is neither.
  • They conflate use frequency with use quality — the how much with the how.
  • They rely on single-level analyses that obscure the multi-level dynamics through which AI impact emerges.
  • The result: organizations face multi-million-dollar adoption decisions with no evidence-based framework for justification, usage design, or fit assessment.

Research question. How does GenAI use affect software delivery performance, and under what conditions does the effect vary?

Falsifiable claims

Hypotheses

  • H1 — Routinization → Throughput. Routinized GenAI use raises throughput, with diminishing returns (linear and quadratic forms specified a priori).
  • H2 — Faithful Appropriation → Stability. Faithful use improves stability beyond routinization alone, via review discipline and selective application.
  • H3 — Task–Technology Fit moderates both relationships; the direct TTF→performance path is tested exploratorily.
  • H1c / H2c — Cross-effects. Their (a)symmetry decides whether separation holds in strict, dominant-but-not-exclusive, or rejected form.

Finding 1 · Routinization

Throughput follows an inverted-U

  • Linear model: a misleading near-zero null (β = −0.025, p = .057).
  • Quadratic model: a strong inverted-U — rising term (β₁ = +0.216, p < .001), falling term (β₂ = −0.030, p = .001).
  • Peak benefit at ~3.6 months of cumulative routinization, then decline.
  • H1c null (p = .644): the dynamic does not extend to quality — separation holds on the routinization side.
Dose–response: effects on DORA metrics by AI-intensity bin, relative to no AI (95% CI).
Finding 2 · Faithful Appropriation

One composite, two opposing mechanisms

−

Review discipline

β = −0.045 on CFR · p = .027

The quality gate works as theorized: gatekeeping catches defects before they ship.

+

Iterative refinement

β = +0.144 on CFR · p = .004

Signals reactive debugging, not proactive refinement — developers iterate after problems surface.

The opposing components cancel in the composite (a classic suppression pattern). H2 is partially supported: review discipline operates as theorized, but the two-indicator FAI does not behave as a unitary construct.

Finding 3 · The empirical anchor

Task–technology fit works directly, not as a moderator

  • Moderation is null (p = .089, p = .712) — H3 as stated is not supported.
  • But the exploratory direct path is the strongest finding in the study: TTF lowers change failure rate, β = −0.307, p < .001.
  • Bugs-only effect is ~3× larger (β = −0.880) — TTF prevents bugs upstream, not just reverts.
  • Developer experience is the active dimension — reconceptualized as a fit dimension, not a context-free moderator.
Predicted change failure rate by routinization × FAI in the high-TTF subsample.
Finding 4 · Not all AI is alike

Agentic vs. assistive tools diverge

  • Agentic AI (Claude Code, Codex, Devin) is associated with lower change failure rate at the org level (β = −0.021, p = .015) — the second-strongest org-level finding.
  • Assistive AI (Copilot, Cursor, ChatGPT) shows a null quality effect (β = −0.002, p = .629).
  • Five of seven tools lift merged-PR volume by +21% to +29%.
  • Amazon Q is the lone speed–quality tradeoff: +88% throughput and +7.1pp failure rate.

Implication: teams with stronger task-routing governance capture agentic tools' advantage; looser teams may benefit more from assistive deployments.

The methodological headline

The ecological fallacy in AI-productivity research

Re-estimated within-developer (1.3M observations), the organizational story reverses:

⇄
Throughput — org-level positive flips to individual-level negative (β = −0.055, p = .013)
⇄
Lead time — org-level longer flips to individual-level shorter (β = −0.108, p = .001)
→0
Agentic quality benefit attenuates to null within developers (p = .784)

Selection, composition, and cohort effects drive the divergence. Single-level designs cannot detect cross-level reconfiguration — multi-level estimation must become standard practice.

Theoretical contributions

Four contributions to IS theory

1

The Faithful Appropriation Index

Operationalizes a 30-year-old AST construct from behavioral Git traces — at scale, continuously, without surveys.

2

The asymmetric separation thesis

Routinization and appropriation are distinct constructs (r = .087) with different dominant performance consequences.

3

Experience as a TTF dimension

Reconciles contradictory field evidence by restoring Goodhue & Thompson's task–technology–ability alignment.

4

The DORA paradox is a low-fit phenomenon

Throughput-without-stability is specific to low task–technology fit — not a universal consequence of AI.

For engineering leaders

What to do with this

  • Invest in depth, not breadth. A 25.6pp gap separates casual (−7.5%) from heavy (+18.1%) users — shallow rollouts can reduce aggregate productivity.
  • Target newer developers first. A 42-month tenure crossover justifies tenure-differentiated deployment.
  • Monitor review discipline alongside throughput. Review depth matters more than breadth; pressure on speed short-circuits the quality gate.
  • Differentiate tools by task and governance. Agentic where routing is strong; assistive where it is loose.
Honesty about scope

Limitations & boundary conditions

  • Measurement. Silent AI adoption is unobserved; the FAI captures behavioral signals, not the cognitive substance of appropriation.
  • Generalizability. Open-source review cultures may differ from policy-mandated enterprise review; the individual panel is visibility-selected.
  • Identification. Despite event studies, IV, PSM, and placebo tests, the design remains observational.
  • Temporal scope. The agentic-AI era spans only six months — cross-level findings should be replicated as it matures.

AI's impact on knowledge work is not a question of adoption — but of how human and machine capability are governed and made to coexist.

Routinization bends throughput in an inverted-U. Faithful appropriation splits into opposing mechanisms. Task–technology fit works directly. And the org-level story reverses within developers.
